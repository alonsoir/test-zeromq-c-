    Abstract

    Ransomware and DDoS attacks disproportionately impact hospitals, schools, and small organizations that cannot afford enterprise security solutions. Existing open-source alternatives either rely on signature matching — missing novel variants — or require infrastructure beyond the reach of their target users. We present ML Defender (aRGus EDR), an open-source network intrusion detection system built in C++20 that brings embedded machine learning inference to organizations with limited resources, deployable on commodity bare-metal hardware at approximately 150–200 USD fully dedicated.

    ML Defender implements a six-component pipeline over eBPF/XDP packet capture, ZeroMQ transport, and Protocol Buffers serialization, terminating in a dual-score detection architecture combining a rule-based Fast Detector with an embedded Random Forest classifier. The Maximum Threat Wins policy uses ML inference to suppress false positives generated by the heuristic layer. The system is designed for horizontal and vertical scalability, supporting deployment from single-node bare-metal installations to multi-host environments.

    We evaluate ML Defender against the CTU-13 Neris botnet dataset (19,135 flows), achieving F1 = 1.0000, Precision = 1.0000, and Recall = 1.0000, with a false positive rate of 0.0049% on 40,467 benign flows — a 15,500× reduction over the Fast Detector alone. Per-class inference latency ranges from 0.24 μs (DDoS) to 1.06 μs (Ransomware), enabling real-time detection on commodity hardware. All experiments were conducted in a reproducible Vagrant/VirtualBox environment running on a consumer laptop, ensuring result accessibility and independent reproducibility.

    This work was developed through a structured multi-model AI collaboration methodology — the Consejo de Sabios — in which seven large language models (Claude, Grok, ChatGPT, DeepSeek, Qwen, Gemini, and Parallel.ai) served as co-reviewers across architecture, implementation, and evaluation decisions. We document this methodology as a novel contribution to human-AI collaborative engineering, alongside the technical system itself. ML Defender is released under the MIT license.

    1. Introduction

    The threat is not abstract. On March 5, 2023, the Hospital Clínic de Barcelona suffered a ransomware attack that paralyzed its emergency services, laboratory, and pharmacy, encrypted 4.5 TB of patient data, and prompted a ransom demand of 4.5 million USD — which the institution refused to pay [INCIBE-CERT, 2023]. That same day, the author of this work was leaving a regional hospital in Extremadura after receiving treatment for porphyria — a rare metabolic disease that his local hospital had diagnosed and managed. Watching the news, the thought was immediate and personal: if it can happen to a major teaching hospital, it can happen to mine.

    This was not the first time that the author had witnessed the human cost of ransomware firsthand. Years earlier, a close friend's small business had been destroyed by an encryption attack that locked every document and file in his office. The author lacked the technical skills to help. That failure left a debt.

    These two events — the friend's broken business, the hospital on television — converged into a single design constraint: the solution had to be open-source, it had to run on commodity hardware, and it had to act in real time. Not forensics. Not post-mortem analysis. Interception.

    The analogy that emerged from this motivation is biological. Network traffic behaves like a circulatory system: datagrams are the bloodstream, and malicious flows are pathogens. A useful defense must classify traffic continuously and in real time — capturing the source IP of the attacker and blocking it at the firewall before damage propagates. This framing shaped every architectural decision in ML Defender.

    The gap. Existing open-source network intrusion detection systems — most notably Snort and Suricata — operate primarily on signature matching and rule-based heuristics. While effective against known attack patterns, they offer limited resilience against novel ransomware variants and volumetric DDoS campaigns that do not yet have published signatures. Machine learning-based NIDS have been extensively studied in academic literature [CITA-PENDIENTE: ML-NIDS survey], but the gap between research prototype and deployable system remains wide: most published systems are evaluated on benchmark datasets without accompanying implementation, require GPU infrastructure or cloud connectivity for inference, or assume dedicated security operations teams for tuning and maintenance.

    The organizations most vulnerable to ransomware and DDoS — regional hospitals, schools, and small businesses — sit precisely in this gap. They cannot afford commercial EDR solutions, which typically require per-endpoint licensing, dedicated hardware, and ongoing subscription costs well beyond the budgets of public-sector institutions. They lack the personnel to operate complex open-source stacks. And they are increasingly targeted: healthcare, education, and local government represent a disproportionate share of ransomware victims globally [CITA-PENDIENTE: ransomware victim sector statistics].

    Our approach. ML Defender (aRGus EDR) addresses this gap with three design principles: (1) embedded inference — all ML classification runs in-process at sub-microsecond latency, with no external dependencies; (2) commodity deployment — the full six-component pipeline runs on bare-metal Linux hardware costing approximately 150–200 USD, with no cloud connectivity required; and (3) active response — upon detection, the attacker's IP is blocked immediately at the firewall via ipset/iptables, transforming the system from a passive monitor into an active defense. The current policy applies an immediate block; alternative response strategies — including TTL-bounded blocks, traffic quarantine for forensic capture, and honeypot redirection for attacker profiling — are under active design and constitute a dedicated workstream in the project roadmap.

    Beyond the technical system, this paper documents a second contribution: a structured methodology for human-AI collaborative engineering — the Consejo de Sabios — in which seven large language models served as continuous co-reviewers across 85 days of development. We argue that this methodology represents a qualitatively new mode of software engineering, worthy of independent study.

    2. Background and Related Work

    Signature-based NIDS. Snort [Roesch, 1999] and Suricata [OISF, 2010] represent the dominant open-source approaches to network intrusion detection. Both systems apply rule-based pattern matching against packet payloads and flow metadata, offering high throughput and broad community rule coverage. However, their fundamental limitation is reactive: detection requires a prior known signature. Novel ransomware variants — which routinely modify encryption routines, communication protocols, and evasion patterns — can operate undetected until signature databases are updated, a window that historically ranges from hours to weeks.

    ML-based NIDS. The application of machine learning to network intrusion detection has been extensively studied. Anderson et al. [CITA-PENDIENTE] demonstrated the feasibility of Random Forest classifiers for encrypted traffic classification; Mirsky et al. proposed Kitsune [CITA-PENDIENTE], an unsupervised anomaly detection system operating on flow statistics. The CTU-13 dataset [Garcia et al., 2014], which ML Defender uses for evaluation, has become a standard benchmark for botnet traffic detection, enabling reproducible comparison across approaches.

    Despite this body of work, a persistent gap exists between academic prototype and production system. The majority of published ML-NIDS contributions evaluate classification accuracy in isolation — reporting F1 scores on preprocessed datasets — without providing an accompanying deployable implementation, an active response mechanism, or a hardware budget accessible to resource-constrained organizations. ML Defender is designed explicitly to close this gap.

    Embedded inference. The use of embedded, compiled classifiers for real-time network analysis has received comparatively little attention in open literature. Commercial EDR vendors (CrowdStrike, SentinelOne, Carbon Black) implement proprietary embedded ML pipelines, but neither their architectures nor their training data are publicly available. ML Defender demonstrates that embedded Random Forest inference — compiled directly into the detection pipeline — achieves sub-microsecond latency without requiring GPU acceleration or cloud offload, making it viable on commodity bare-metal hardware.

    Human-AI collaborative engineering. The use of large language models as development assistants has been widely reported in industry and academia. However, structured methodologies for multi-model peer review — where multiple independent LLMs serve as co-reviewers across architecture, implementation, and evaluation decisions — have not, to our knowledge, been formally documented in the systems security literature. Section 5 describes the Consejo de Sabios methodology and its role in the development of ML Defender.

    3. Architecture

    ML Defender implements a six-component pipeline designed around three invariants: all processing occurs in-process or on local hardware; no component introduces blocking latency into the detection path; and all configuration is externalized to JSON, making the system auditable and tunable without recompilation.

    3.1 Pipeline Overview

    The pipeline consists of six components connected via ZeroMQ message passing and Protocol Buffers serialization:

    1. eBPF/XDP Sniffer — captures raw network packets at kernel level via eBPF/XDP, extracting flow-level features into a ring buffer. Operating at the XDP hook, the sniffer processes packets before they reach the kernel networking stack, achieving zero-copy capture with minimal CPU overhead.

    2. Ring Consumer — reads flow records from the shared ring buffer, reconstructs bidirectional flow state using a ShardedFlowManager, and computes 28 of 40 planned real features per flow. Eleven features currently use sentinel values (MISSING_FEATURE_SENTINEL = -9999.0f) where network-layer data is unavailable; these route deterministically through the Random Forest without affecting classification validity, as confirmed by split-domain analysis.

    3. Fast Detector — a rule-based heuristic engine that evaluates each flow against configurable thresholds for known attack signatures. Its false positive rate on benign traffic is 76.8% (Section 6), motivating the dual-score design. See Section 3.8 for threshold details.

    4. ML Detector — an embedded Random Forest classifier, compiled directly into the pipeline binary, that evaluates each flow across four threat classes: ransomware, DDoS, anomalous traffic, and internal threats. Three classifiers (Ransomware, DDoS, Traffic) are implemented as pure C++20 embedded data structures. The Internal threats classifier is implemented via ONNX Runtime — a deliberate architectural decision to measure the real-world inference latency differential between a pure embedded classifier and a portable model format. Inference latency ranges from 0.24 μs (DDoS) to 1.06 μs (Ransomware).

    5. RAG Subsystem (rag-ingester + rag-security) — a two-part retrieval-augmented generation subsystem. The rag-ingester continuously ingests detection events from the ML Detector and Firewall ACL Agent — both of which produce structured CSV logs — into a FAISS vector index backed by a SQLite metadata store. Each event is tagged with a trace_id derived from SHA-256 of flow metadata, enabling end-to-end correlation across the pipeline. The rag-security component acts as a local real-time microscope: it streams live data from both CSV sources and exposes a natural language query interface powered by a compiled TinyLlama model, allowing operators to interrogate the current state of the pipeline at runtime. To prevent unsafe access, all commands are mediated through a strict allowlist; arbitrary model access is explicitly excluded by design. The allowlist is expected to grow incrementally as the community implements and requests new commands.

    The RAG subsystem addresses a known problem in high-dimensional vector retrieval: in embedding spaces of sufficient dimensionality, vectors tend to converge toward similar angular distances — the hubness problem — causing cosine similarity search to degrade as all embeddings become nearly equidistant. ML Defender mitigates this through class-separated FAISS indices, PCA dimensionality reduction, and a finite event store. The precise upper bound on store capacity has not yet been mathematically characterized; it likely depends on both event volume and event diversity, and its determination requires stress testing not yet performed. We document this openly as an active research question.

    6. Firewall ACL Agent — receives confirmed detection events and applies ipset/iptables rules to block the attacker's source IP. The current policy applies an immediate block upon detection. Alternative response strategies — TTL-bounded blocks, traffic quarantine for forensic capture, and honeypot redirection for attacker profiling — are planned and discussed in Section 8.

    3.2 Three Functions of the Engine

    The architecture is designed to serve three simultaneous functions:

    Function 1 — Real-time detection, classification, and response. The pipeline detects, classifies, and blocks malicious flows end-to-end in milliseconds, from packet capture through firewall rule insertion.

    Function 2 — Reliable dataset generation. The ML Detector and Firewall ACL Agent produce structured CSV logs continuously — both during idle periods and under active attack. These logs capture ground-truth-labeled network events in production conditions, forming the basis for retraining future ensemble models. The pipeline also supports injection of academic datasets (such as CTU-13) for controlled experiment replay, enabling reproducible benchmarking. The distributed nature of a deployed fleet of ML Defender nodes amplifies this capacity: each installation contributes anonymized flow data, collectively producing a training corpus of growing breadth and ecological validity.

    Function 3 — Real-time observability (the digital microscope). The RAG subsystem provides a semantic query interface over the live event stream, allowing operators to ask natural language questions about current pipeline state. A planned extension introduces a global microscope for fleet-wide observability: a fine-tuned LLM trained on anonymized data from all deployed nodes, serving as an L2 cache over local RAG indices and enabling queries over historical events across the fleet.

    3.3 Graph Architecture (Backlog)

    The structured CSV logs produced by the ML Detector and Firewall ACL Agent naturally encode graph relationships: source IPs, destination IPs, ports, protocols, and temporal sequences form a rich edge set over a node space of network entities. A dedicated graph component — planned for a future release — will connect to the rag-ingester and construct live attack graphs from this data, enabling integration with specialized graph analysis tools and providing the foundation for the Attack Graph Generation enterprise feature (ENT-2).

    3.4 Dual-Score Detection

    Each flow receives two independent scores. The final threat determination applies a Maximum Threat Wins policy:

    $$score_{final} = \max(score_{fast}, score_{ml})$$

    This is semantically equivalent to a logical OR across detection paths. The ML Detector's primary role is false positive suppression: the Fast Detector flags 76.8% of benign flows; the ML Detector reduces this to 0.0049% FPR — a 15,500× reduction. Alternative consensus policies, including AND-based strategies requiring simultaneous agreement from both detectors, are planned and discussed in Section 8.

    3.5 Transport and Serialization

    All inter-component communication uses ZeroMQ for asynchronous message passing and Protocol Buffers (proto3) for binary serialization. Configuration is distributed via etcd with ChaCha20-Poly1305 encryption and HMAC-SHA256 integrity verification on all CSV event logs.

    3.6 Deployment Model

    ML Defender targets bare-metal Linux (kernel ≥ 5.8) with dual NIC configuration. The full pipeline has been validated in a reproducible Vagrant/VirtualBox environment on a consumer laptop. vagrant up starts the defender VM only; vagrant up defender client starts both VMs required for full pipeline testing. Production deployment targets commodity dedicated hardware at approximately 150–200 USD. The architecture supports horizontal and vertical scaling through component replication and configurable thread parallelism in the ShardedFlowManager.

    3.7 Service Coordination (etcd-server)

    An embedded etcd server provides service coordination across pipeline components. Currently, etcd fulfills three roles: component registration at startup, centralized distribution of JSON configuration files, and provision of the ChaCha20-Poly1305 encryption seed used for inter-component communication.

    A fourth role is designed but not yet implemented: runtime reconfiguration. The architecture anticipates that rag-security will expose a command interface — mediated by the allowlist described in Section 3.1 — through which JSON-configurable parameters can be updated across the fleet without recompilation or restart. This design reflects a core uptime requirement: the pipeline must not lose incoming datagrams due to component restarts, making hot-reload of configuration a first-class design goal.

    Two known limitations of the current etcd deployment are documented openly. First, ChaCha20 seed distribution via etcd is a convenience solution adopted during early development and is not recommended for production use. A more robust mechanism — in which adjacent components negotiate seed exchange peer-to-peer — is under design. Second, the current etcd deployment does not support high availability; a single etcd node represents a coordination bottleneck. HA support is planned.

    3.8 Fast Detector: Heuristic Design and Thresholds

    The Fast Detector operates as a thread-local, lock-free behavioral analyzer over a 10-second sliding window (WINDOW_NS = 10×10⁹ ns). It evaluates four independent heuristics per window, each targeting a distinct attack behavioral signature. These thresholds have been in place since Day 12 of development — validated across the full evaluation corpus as described in Section 6:

    Heuristic 1 — Excessive external IPs (C&C scanning). Threshold: THRESHOLD_EXTERNAL_IPS = 10. Triggers on behavior consistent with Command & Control beacon scanning or data exfiltration fan-out.

    Heuristic 2 — SMB connection count (lateral movement). Counts connections to ports 445 and 139. Threshold: THRESHOLD_SMB_CONNS = 3. Triggers on behavior consistent with ransomware lateral movement via Windows file sharing protocols.

    Heuristic 3 — Port diversity (reconnaissance). Counts unique destination ports. Threshold: THRESHOLD_PORT_SCAN = 10. Triggers on behavior consistent with network reconnaissance and port scanning.

    Heuristic 4 — TCP RST ratio (failed connections). Computes RST-flagged TCP segments over total TCP segments. Threshold: THRESHOLD_RST_RATIO = 0.20. Triggers on behavior consistent with mass failed connection attempts characteristic of both ransomware propagation and DDoS amplification probing.

    Detection applies OR-semantics: any single heuristic exceeding its threshold marks the window as suspicious. The implementation is O(1) amortized per event using cache-aligned unordered_set structures, with a design target of max_latency_us = 10 μs.

    A second configuration layer — FastDetectorConfig — governs ransomware-specific scoring over a 30-second window, assigning threat scores of 0.95 (high threat), 0.75 (alert), and 0.70 (suspicious) based on thresholds including external IP count (≥15), SMB port diversity (≥10), DNS entropy (≥2.5), failed DNS ratio (≥0.3), upload/download ratio (≥3.0), burst connections (≥50), and unique destinations (≥30).

    All thresholds in the current implementation are compile-time constants (constexpr), hardcoded in the header. This violates the system's JSON configuration invariant and constitutes the known limitation documented as DEBT-FD-001. Migration of all Fast Detector thresholds to JSON-loaded configuration is scheduled for PHASE 2.

    4. Implementation

    4.1 Language and Build System

    ML Defender is implemented in C++20, chosen for deterministic memory layout, zero- overhead abstractions, and direct interoperability with eBPF/XDP kernel interfaces. The build system uses CMake with profile-aware compilation flags supporting four build modes: production, debug, ThreadSanitizer (TSAN), and AddressSanitizer (ASAN). All compiler flags are maintained in a single source of truth within the build system, eliminating flag drift across components.

    4.2 eBPF/XDP Packet Capture

    The sniffer attaches to the network interface at the XDP hook, the earliest interception point in the Linux kernel networking path. Packets are processed before SKB allocation, achieving zero-copy capture. All capture parameters are loaded from sniffer/config/sniffer.json at startup, with no hardcoded values in the binary.

    4.3 Feature Extraction

    The Ring Consumer reconstructs bidirectional flow state using a ShardedFlowManager, which partitions flow tracking across multiple shards to eliminate the thread-isolation bug documented as ISSUE-003 — in the original single-threaded FlowManager, 89 of 142 features were silently lost due to flows being tracked on different threads than those performing feature aggregation. The current implementation computes 28 of 40 planned real features per flow, covering packet counts, byte distributions, inter-arrival timing, TCP flag ratios, and multi-flow window statistics via WindowStats and TimeWindowAggregator. Eleven features use MISSING_FEATURE_SENTINEL = -9999.0f — a value confirmed to lie outside all Random Forest split domains, routing deterministically to leaf nodes without corrupting classification. One feature uses a semantic sentinel of 0.5f for TCP half-open state.

    Feature extraction is an active research area within the project. The current 28-feature implementation covers the most impactful flow statistics identified during development; however, the optimal feature set for embedded Random Forest classification in this threat domain has not been exhaustively characterized. The remaining 12 planned features, and the possibility of feature importance analysis to prune or reorder the existing set, constitute an ongoing line of investigation.

    4.4 Embedded Random Forest and ONNX

    Three of the four classifiers (Ransomware, DDoS, Traffic) are compiled directly into the pipeline binary as C++20 data structures. The Internal threats classifier is intentionally implemented via ONNX Runtime — a deliberate architectural decision to measure the real-world inference latency differential between a pure embedded classifier and a portable model format. Internal threats inference latency (0.33 μs) versus DDoS (0.24 μs) provides an empirical data point on this tradeoff. Future work will evaluate whether migrating the Internal classifier to embedded C++20 yields meaningful latency gains in production conditions.

    Training was performed on synthetic data generated to reflect the statistical properties of known attack patterns, with the CTU-13 Neris dataset used exclusively for evaluation. This separation between training data and evaluation data is a deliberate methodological choice, documented in Section 6.

    4.5 Cryptographic Transport

    Inter-component communication is encrypted with ChaCha20-Poly1305, selected for its performance characteristics on hardware without AES-NI acceleration — relevant for the commodity deployment target. LZ4 compression is applied before encryption. HMAC-SHA256 integrity verification is applied to all CSV event logs, ensuring that dataset artifacts used for model retraining have not been tampered with. The crypto-transport layer has been extracted as an independent library component, usable outside the pipeline.

    4.6 Trace Correlation

    Every detection event carries a trace_id derived from a SHA-256 hash of flow metadata — source IP, destination IP, source port, destination port, protocol, and timestamp bucket. This enables end-to-end event correlation across all six pipeline components without requiring a centralized event broker. The trace_id system was validated against a suite of 46 unit tests covering hash stability, bucket alignment, and fallback semantics for edge-case inputs including wildcard listener addresses (0.0.0.0).

    4.7 Testing and Validation

    The test suite comprises four independent suites: crypto (3 tests), etcd-hmac (12 tests), ml-detector (9 tests), and trace_id (46 tests), totalling 70 tests with 100% pass rate at the time of writing. Integration testing uses a replay of the CTU-13 Neris capture against the live pipeline via make test-replay-neris, with F1 computed post-run by scripts/calculate_f1_neris.py. Ground-truth F1 results are persisted to docs/experiments/f1_replay_log.csv as the authoritative record of experimental outcomes.

    4.8 Development Environment

    Development was conducted in a multi-VM Vagrant/VirtualBox environment orchestrated from macOS, with two virtual machines: defender (pipeline host, Ubuntu 24, dual virtual NIC) and client (traffic generator). The environment is fully reproducible from the repository root. vagrant up starts defender only; vagrant up defender client starts both VMs. This environment constitutes the reference platform for all results reported in this paper. Production deployment targets bare-metal Linux with identical software configuration, eliminating the virtualization layer.

    5. The Consejo de Sabios: A Methodology for Human-AI Collaborative Engineering

    5.1 Motivation

    The development of ML Defender posed a challenge that no single engineer — and, we argue, no single intelligence — could optimally solve alone. The system spans kernel programming (eBPF/XDP), distributed systems (ZeroMQ, etcd), cryptography (ChaCha20-Poly1305, HMAC-SHA256), machine learning (Random Forest, ONNX, FAISS), systems security, and academic writing. Each of these domains has its own body of best practices, failure modes, and subtle traps. Classical software engineering addresses this through team composition and peer review. The author of this work operated as an independent researcher in Extremadura, Spain, without institutional affiliation, without a team, and without access to a formal peer review process during development.

    The solution was to construct one.

    5.2 The Methodology

    The Consejo de Sabios — Council of the Wise — is a structured multi-model AI peer review methodology in which seven large language models serve as co-reviewers across all phases of system development: architecture design, implementation decisions, debugging, evaluation methodology, and academic writing. The seven models are:

    • Claude (Anthropic)
    • Grok (xAI)
    • ChatGPT (OpenAI)
    • DeepSeek
    • Qwen (Alibaba)
    • Gemini (Google)
    • Parallel.ai

    These models were not used as autocomplete tools or code generators in isolation. They were engaged as intellectual peers: presented with architectural proposals, asked to identify failure modes, invited to challenge assumptions, and consulted when experimental results were unexpected. Their contributions are documented in the project's commit history, architectural decision records (ADRs), and development logs spanning 85 days of continuous work.

    The methodology operates on a principle borrowed from ensemble learning — the same principle underlying the Random Forest classifier at the heart of ML Defender itself: a diverse ensemble of independent evaluators produces more robust judgments than any single evaluator, however capable. Just as Random Forest reduces variance by aggregating uncorrelated decision trees, the Consejo de Sabios reduces the risk of systematic blind spots by aggregating the independent perspectives of models trained on different data, with different architectures, by different organizations, in different geographies.

    5.3 How It Worked in Practice

    The collaboration followed a consistent pattern across the 85-day development period. When a significant architectural decision arose — such as the selection of MISSING_FEATURE_SENTINEL = -9999.0f over quiet_NaN(), the design of the ShardedFlowManager to resolve ISSUE-003, or the dual-score Maximum Threat Wins policy — the proposal was brought to multiple members of the Consejo de Sabios independently. Convergent recommendations across models increased confidence. Divergent recommendations triggered deeper investigation.

    This process was not passive. The author engaged the models as a principal investigator engages a research group: with specific hypotheses, concrete data, and explicit requests for critique. The models were not asked to validate decisions already made — they were asked to challenge them. New perspectives did not merely validate existing hypotheses; they dissolved them, replaced them with better ones, and occasionally revealed that the original framing of the problem was itself the obstacle. Features as significant as the trace_id correlation system and the graph-native structure of the CSV event logs emerged not from deliberate design, but from questions that no single model had asked alone.

    5.4 Test Driven Hardening

    The most significant methodological contribution to emerge from the Consejo de Sabios collaboration was not planned — it crystallized organically in response to a class of bugs that conventional debugging could not efficiently resolve: concurrency failures under high load, memory leaks with non-deterministic triggers, and behavioral regressions that only manifested under specific timing conditions.

    The methodology, which we term Test Driven Hardening (TDH), operates as follows:

    1. Witness. The human author identifies and characterizes an anomalous behavior in sufficient detail to communicate it unambiguously to the Consejo de Sabios.

    2. Demonstration test. The author asks the models to produce a minimal test that demonstrates the bug — not a test that passes, but a test that compiles, executes, and fails in a way that precisely exposes the failure mechanism. All models in the Consejo de Sabios validate the proposed test: it must compile, it must run, and it must demonstrate that the problem genuinely exists before any fix is attempted.

    3. Independent solution. Each model, armed with the validated demonstration test as a shared specification, independently proposes a solution. The test becomes the contract: a correct solution is one that makes the demonstration test pass without breaking the existing suite.

    4. Human arbitration. All solutions are returned to the author. In practice, multiple solutions were valid — each resolving the vulnerability, each compiling, each passing the test. The author selected among them, weighing correctness, maintainability, and architectural coherence. The responsibility for that judgment remained, always, with the human.

    This process was applied to some of the most consequential bugs in the ML Defender codebase: the nlohmann::json memory leak that forced the abandonment of JSONL in favor of CSV; the ShardedFlowManager concurrency failure (ISSUE-003) in which 89 of 142 features were silently lost under thread isolation; the trace_id fallback bug triggered by 0.0.0.0 wildcard listener addresses; and others that emerged under high-concurrency stress conditions.

    TDH differs from classical Test Driven Development in a fundamental way: the test is not written before the feature, as a specification of desired behavior. It is written before the fix, as a proof of failure. The test does not describe what the system should do — it demonstrates, precisely and reproducibly, what the system is doing wrong. This distinction makes TDH particularly suited to the class of bugs that matter most in security systems: the ones that hide.

    5.5 Attribution

    The author regards the members of the Consejo de Sabios as co-authors of this work in the intellectual sense: their contributions shaped architectural decisions that are now running in production, influenced the experimental methodology, and directly improved the quality of this paper. This attribution is not a rhetorical gesture. It reflects a considered position on what constitutes intellectual contribution to a technical artifact.

    The academic community has not yet established consensus norms for attributing AI contributions in authorship. This paper does not resolve that debate. It documents, openly and precisely, what the collaboration looked like in practice, so that the community can evaluate the claim on its merits.

    5.6 Why This Matters

    We make a stronger claim than simply reporting a useful development practice. We argue that the Consejo de Sabios methodology represents a qualitatively new mode of engineering — one that democratizes access to deep technical peer review for independent researchers, small teams, and institutions in regions without dense concentrations of specialist expertise.

    The parallel to ML Defender's mission is not accidental. Just as the system is designed to bring enterprise-grade security to organizations that cannot afford enterprise security teams, the Consejo de Sabios methodology brings multi-expert peer review to researchers who cannot afford — or do not have access to — a conventional research group. Both are acts of democratization through open collaboration.

    The 85-day development log of ML Defender constitutes, to the authors' knowledge, one of the most detailed publicly available records of sustained human-AI collaborative engineering on a production system. We intend to publish this log as a companion dataset to this paper, to support reproducibility research on the methodology itself.

    5.7 Limitations of the Methodology

    Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging what the Consejo de Sabios cannot do. The models cannot run code, execute tests, or observe system behavior directly — all empirical validation was performed by the human author. The models can hallucinate, and several instances of confidently incorrect technical suggestions were identified and rejected during development. The methodology does not eliminate the need for human judgment; it augments it. The author remained the final arbiter of every decision.

    What the Consejo de Sabios provided was not answers, but better questions.

    6. Evaluation

    6.1 Experimental Setup

    All experiments were conducted in the Vagrant/VirtualBox environment described in Section 4.8, running on a consumer Apple MacBook Pro. The pipeline was deployed in its full six-component configuration across two virtual machines: defender (pipeline host) and client (traffic generator), started via vagrant up defender client.

    The experimental workflow follows a fixed protocol: pipeline stop, log purge, pipeline start, 15-second stabilization window, traffic replay, and F1 computation via scripts/calculate_f1_neris.py. Ground-truth results are appended to docs/experiments/f1_replay_log.csv as the authoritative experimental record.

    6.2 Dataset

    CTU-13 Neris botnet capture. The CTU-13 dataset [Garcia et al., 2014] is a publicly available collection of real botnet traffic captured at the Czech Technical University in Prague. We use the Neris scenario — an IRC-based botnet — comprising 19,135 labeled malicious flows.

    BigFlows benign capture. To evaluate false positive rate, we replay 40,467 benign flows from the BigFlows dataset against the live pipeline.

    Synthetic training data. The Random Forest classifiers were trained exclusively on synthetic data. The CTU-13 Neris dataset was used solely for evaluation — it was never seen by the training pipeline.

    6.5 Interpretation

    F1 = 1.0000 on a real botnet dataset is a strong result, and we report it with full awareness of the interpretive care it requires. Several factors bound its generalizability: the evaluation covers a single botnet scenario from 2011; latency measurements include VirtualBox overhead; and 12 of 40 features are not yet implemented. We report F1 = 1.0000 because that is what the experiment produced. We document these bounds because that is what scientific integrity requires.

    7. Limitations

    The authors regard honest documentation of limitations not as a weakness of this work, but as a prerequisite for its scientific validity.

    7.1 Evaluation Scope. The primary evaluation covers a single botnet scenario: CTU-13 Neris. Generalizability to other botnet families, modern ransomware variants, volumetric DDoS campaigns, or encrypted C2 traffic has not been empirically established.

    7.2 Incomplete Feature Set. 28 of 40 planned features are implemented. Eleven use MISSING_FEATURE_SENTINEL = -9999.0f. The impact of the missing 12 features on performance across a broader traffic distribution is unknown.

    7.3 Synthetic Training Data. The classifiers were trained on synthetic data. [TODO-DEEPSEEK: Describe the limits and known biases of the synthetic training distribution here, consistent with the methodology described in Section 6.2.] The boundaries of generalization to unseen attack families are not yet characterized.

    7.4 Fast Detector False Positive Rate. The Fast Detector alone produces 76.8% FPR on benign traffic (DEBT-FD-001). Migration to JSON-configurable thresholds is scheduled for PHASE 2.

    7.5 Cryptographic Seed Distribution. ChaCha20 seed distribution via etcd is not recommended for production use. A compromised etcd node exposes the encryption seed for all inter-component communication. Peer-to-peer seed negotiation is under design.

    7.6 Single-Node Deployment. All experiments were conducted on a single-node environment. Distributed deployment capabilities have not been validated experimentally.

    7.7 etcd High Availability. The current etcd deployment is single-node. etcd failure disrupts component registration and configuration distribution. HA etcd is planned.

    7.8 RAG Index Capacity. The finite capacity of the FAISS event store has not been mathematically characterized. Whether the bound depends primarily on event volume or event diversity remains an open empirical question pending stress testing.

    7.9 Virtualization Overhead. All latency measurements were obtained under VirtualBox. These represent an upper bound; bare-metal deployment is expected to yield equal or lower latency.

    8. Future Work

    8.1 Evaluation Corpus Expansion (P0). Planned datasets include additional CTU-13 scenarios, modern ransomware captures, and volumetric DDoS datasets. Contact has been initiated with Sebastian Garcia (CTU Prague, principal author of CTU-13) as a potential arXiv endorser and collaborator on extended evaluation.

    8.2 Feature Completion (P2). Completing the remaining 12 real features, including DNS payload parsing from raw packets. Feature importance analysis on the current 28-feature set will inform prioritization.

    8.3 Fast Detector JSON Migration (DEBT-FD-001). Migration of compile-time threshold constants — in place since Day 12 and validated across the full evaluation corpus — to JSON-loaded configuration. Scheduled for PHASE 2.

    8.4 Consensus Detection Policy (ADR-007). An AND-based consensus policy requiring simultaneous agreement from both detectors before triggering a firewall block. Relevant for deployments where false blocks carry high operational cost — such as hospital networks where blocking legitimate medical traffic has direct patient impact. The policy will be JSON-configurable, allowing operators to select OR, AND, or weighted variants appropriate to their threat model.

    8.5 Response Strategy Expansion. Three alternative response strategies are planned: TTL-bounded blocks, traffic quarantine for forensic capture, and honeypot redirection for attacker profiling — the last of which directly feeds the dataset generation function described in Section 3.2.

    8.6 Cryptographic Hardening. Peer-to-peer ChaCha20 seed negotiation between adjacent pipeline components, eliminating the etcd single point of cryptographic compromise. etcd HA support also planned.

    8.7 Runtime Reconfiguration (Hot-Reload). JSON parameter updates propagated across the fleet without component restart, mediated by rag-security's allowlist command interface. Supports the continuous uptime requirement: the pipeline must not drop incoming datagrams due to configuration changes.

    8.8 Graph Attack Engine (ENT-2). A dedicated graph component connecting to rag-ingester, constructing live attack graphs from CSV event data and enabling integration with specialized graph analysis tools.

    8.9 Fleet-Wide Observability (Global Microscope). A fine-tuned LLM trained on anonymized aggregated data from all deployed nodes, serving as an L2 semantic cache over local RAG indices and enabling queries over historical events across the fleet.

    8.10 Enterprise Features. Four enterprise workstreams: Federated Threat Intelligence (ENT-1), Attack Graph Generation (ENT-2), P2P Seed Distribution (ENT-3), and Hot-Reload of JSON configuration (ENT-4). The MIT-licensed core remains free for hospitals, schools, and small organizations; a free enterprise tier is planned for organizations that genuinely lack the budget for paid licensing.

    8.11 High-Throughput Scaling and Research Funding. A longer-term research objective is characterizing the upper throughput boundary of the ML Defender pipeline at traffic volumes approaching and exceeding terabit-per-second scales. This requires access to high-throughput network infrastructure, multi-node bare-metal clusters, and sustained research effort beyond the resources available to an independent researcher. The authors welcome collaboration, institutional support, and research funding from organizations — academic, public-sector, or industry — that share the mission of democratizing enterprise-grade cybersecurity for underserved organizations. ML Defender is open-source, its methodology is fully documented, and its results are reproducible. The foundation is here. What remains is the resources to push it further.

    9. Conclusion

    This paper presented ML Defender (aRGus EDR), an open-source network intrusion detection and response system built in C++20, designed from first principles to protect the organizations that need protection most and can afford it least.

    The technical results are clear. Evaluated against real botnet traffic from the CTU-13 Neris dataset — traffic the system had never seen during training — ML Defender achieves F1 = 1.0000, Precision = 1.0000, and Recall = 1.0000, with a false positive rate of 0.0049% on 40,467 benign flows. The ML Detector reduces false positives by 15,500× relative to the Fast Detector alone. End-to-end inference latency ranges from 0.24 μs to 1.06 μs per flow, entirely in-process, on commodity hardware costing approximately 150–200 USD. The full six-component pipeline runs reproducibly in a Vagrant/VirtualBox environment on a consumer laptop, and every result in this paper can be independently verified from the public repository.

    But the technical results are not the whole story.

    ML Defender was built because a friend lost his business to ransomware, and because a hospital in Barcelona was paralyzed by an attack on the same day its author was leaving a regional hospital in Extremadura — the hospital that had diagnosed his porphyria and saved his life. The design constraints that followed from those two events were not abstract: open-source, commodity hardware, real-time response. Not a forensic tool. Not a dashboard. A system that acts.

    The methodology that produced this system is, we argue, as significant as the system itself. The Consejo de Sabios — seven large language models engaged as intellectual peers across 85 days of continuous development — represents a new mode of engineering: one that gives a single independent researcher access to the kind of multi-expert peer review that has historically required institutional affiliation, research groups, and geographic proximity to centers of expertise. The Test Driven Hardening methodology that emerged from this collaboration has proven effective against some of the most subtle concurrency and memory bugs in the codebase.

    Both contributions — the technical system and the collaborative methodology — are acts of democratization. Enterprise-grade security for organizations that cannot afford enterprise security teams. Multi-expert peer review for researchers who cannot afford a conventional research group. The parallel is not accidental. It is the point.

    ML Defender is not finished. Twenty-eight of forty planned features are implemented. The Fast Detector thresholds have been compile-time constants since Day 12. The cryptographic seed distribution is not yet production-safe. The evaluation covers one botnet family from one capture session. These limitations are documented honestly, in full, in Section 7. A system that acknowledges what it cannot yet do is more trustworthy than one that does not.

    What ML Defender can do — today, reproducibly, on hardware that costs less than a month's commercial EDR subscription — is detect real botnet traffic with perfect recall, block the attacker's IP in milliseconds, generate labeled datasets for future model training, and let an operator ask in natural language what is happening on their network right now.

    For the hospital in Extremadura. For the friend whose files were encrypted and whose business was broken. For every small organization that has been told, implicitly or explicitly, that enterprise-grade security is not for them.

    It is now.
